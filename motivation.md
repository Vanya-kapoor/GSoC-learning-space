# Motivation

## Who I am
I'm a first-year BTech student in Computer Science and AI at NSUT Delhi.
I've been coding for a few years — mostly Python, some competitive programming
(Codeforces ~1000). I'm comfortable with data structures and algorithms but
this is my first time contributing to a real open source project. I box,
play tennis and play the piano when I'm not at a screen.

## Why Mesa
I found Mesa while preparing my GSoC 2026 application. I started by running
every example in mesa-examples against Mesa 3.3.1 to understand what the
codebase actually looked like — not just reading docs. About two-thirds of the
examples were broken, and the failures fell into completely different categories
that weren't obvious from the error messages. That audit is what got me hooked.
I'd never worked with agent-based modeling before but the idea that complex
emergent behaviour comes from simple local rules is genuinely interesting to me.

## What I want to learn
I want to understand how a real open source Python project is maintained — CI,
review processes, API design decisions, how things break across versions. On
the Mesa side specifically: how ContinuousSpace and NetworkGrid work in
practice (which is why I built the two models here), and how the metadata and
CI infrastructure I've been contributing to actually fits into the bigger
revival effort. I also want to get better at reading other people's code
critically — which peer review is forcing me to do.

## Where I want to go
My immediate goal is to get broken examples in mesa-examples working on
Mesa 3.x and contribute infrastructure that makes future breakage visible
before it silently accumulates. Longer term I want to contribute to the
mesa-examples revival as a GSoC project — fixing examples, improving
documentation standards, and making the repo useful for someone completely
new to ABM who needs a working reference, not a broken one.
